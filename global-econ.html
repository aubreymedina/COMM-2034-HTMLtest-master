    • Class Overview

    This course is designed to introduce students to the broad contours of the past and present debates surrounding globalization. In analyzing these debates, students will become familiar with key themes and theories ininternational relations(IR)and international political economy(IPE). The required readings will introduce students to a variety of different economic schools of thought,including classical and neoclassical approaches, as well as more critical branchesof scholarship. These economic schools of thought will be analyzed in tandem with an in-depth examination of the main theoretical perspectives in present-day IPE scholarship. By analyzing the work of prominent authors who hail from neomercantilist, liberal, constructivist, Marxist/neo-Marxist, and feminist schools of thought, this class will shed light on the complex web of public and privaterelationships that current discussions of globalization are centered upon.

    • My Take

    This class is extremely difficult for me. Although I have a pretty solid background in International Studies, right off the bat, Dr. Cutter expected us to know several topics. This was concerning, as the course was thought to be an introductory class to economy and politics. The readings assigned are rather lengthy (30 pages a night) and very dense. There are 4 quizzes throughout the semester, each have less than 10 questions. Although she tells you beforehand which readings the questions will come from, they are still hard because, like I said, the readings are very long and the questions are pretty specific. She's a very kind professor, but if you don't have the time to read chapters a night, I don't reccomend this class.
